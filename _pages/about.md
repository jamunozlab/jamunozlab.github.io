Core research
--
The main question we try to answer in our group is: what happens when you squeeze a material and heat it up? Atoms in solids oscillate about their equilibrium positions because they are repeled by other atoms in their local neighborhood. These atomic displacements provide the main contribution to the entropy of a material and they are critical for thermodynamic stability. Although atomic oscillations are often close to being purely harmonic (the atoms move in a quadratic potential), at least on average, a plethora of deviations appear in almost every material when you look in enough detail. These deviations can be investigated with state-of-the-art experimental and computational techniques and are often due to quantum effects.

The ions in a material get closer together when the material is squeezed with pressure, but the arrangement of the electrons also changes. Atomic vibrations are quantized (called phonons) and patterns of atomic vibrations can interact with electrons and with other degrees of freedom. Pressure therefore changes how atoms vibrate in non-trivial ways. Temperature is related to the kinetic energy of the ions in the system, so at higher temperature the displacements of the atoms about their equilibrium positions are larger. Thermal disorder and large atomic displacements can significantly modify the quadratic potential pictures. The question posed above seems innocuous enough, but it leads to the discovery of new physics.
